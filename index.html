About

A bit about me

I got into this work because I was tired of seeing good programmes struggle to prove they were working. Too many organisations collect data because someone told them to, not because they have a real question they are trying to answer. I help fix that.

My background is in GIS and spatial analysis, which taught me to look past single numbers and see the patterns underneath. Whether I am designing a theory of change, running a matched pair impact analysis, or rebuilding a dashboard that has not been touched in two years, I am always asking: what decision will this evidence actually change?

I am based in Nairobi and have worked across Kenya, Uganda, Nigeria, Ghana, Rwanda and Ethiopia. I am comfortable working remotely, independently, and across time zones. I also write about data, evaluation and AI at @_everythingdata.

How I Think

Frameworks & Methods

I match the method to the question, not the tool to the dataset. Sometimes that is a controlled trial. Sometimes it is outcome harvesting. Sometimes it is building a dashboard that makes the question obvious.

THEORY & DESIGN

Planning Frameworks

WHAT I USE

Theory of Change (DCED standards), Results Frameworks, Logframes, IPTTs, Learning Agendas, and Contribution Analysis (Mayne).

WHEN I USE THEM

At programme design or mid-course review, when teams need to articulate how change happens, define what success looks like, and build a shared language before data collection begins.

EVALUATION

Evaluation Methods

WHAT I USE

Outcome Harvesting (Wilson-Grau), Process Tracing (Beach & Pedersen), Contribution Analysis, Quasi-experimental designs (matched pairs, difference-in-differences), and OECD DAC criteria.

WHEN I USE THEM

When programmes need credible evidence of contribution or attribution, balancing rigour with the practical constraints of real-world implementation.

ACCESS TO FINANCE

Youth Finance Programme / Rwanda

MY ROLE

MEL lead for a youth-focused access to finance initiative in rural Rwanda, designing monitoring systems and tracking finance uptake among young entrepreneurs.

CHALLENGE

Youth in rural Rwanda faced significant barriers accessing formal financial services. The programme needed to track not just loan disbursements but actual business outcomes and repayment behaviour among first-time borrowers.

APPROACH

Built a monitoring framework tracking finance access, business performance and youth employment outcomes. Designed survey instruments capturing both quantitative uptake data and qualitative barriers faced by young entrepreneurs in hard-to-reach areas.

IMPACT

Provided the programme team and stakeholders with evidence on what worked for youth finance access in rural settings, informing adjustments to product design and outreach strategy.

ANALYSIS

FIRST+II Impact Analysis / CapPlus & Mastercard Foundation

MY ROLE

Designed and ran the analysis that underpinned the report's headline findings.

CHALLENGE

The annual impact report needed credible, defensible evidence of business growth in revenue, profit and employment that could withstand scrutiny.

APPROACH

Selected a matched-pair design over simple pre-post comparison to control for selection bias. Used CAGR methodology with IQR-based outlier removal to keep comparisons statistically sound and guard against skew from extreme values.

WHY THIS METHOD

Pre-post comparisons risk confounding from external economic shocks. Matched pairs isolate programme effect by comparing similar beneficiaries and non-beneficiaries, strengthening causal claims under donor scrutiny.

IMPACT

Formed the analytical backbone of the FIRST+II Annual Impact Report delivered to CapPlus and Mastercard Foundation.

RESEARCH

When Measurement Becomes Surveillance

On how tracking systems reshape the behaviour they were meant to observe. When people know they are being measured, they adjust. Over time, what is measured becomes what is optimized, and what is optimized becomes what is valued. External measures begin to replace internal judgment. This is cognitive capture.
